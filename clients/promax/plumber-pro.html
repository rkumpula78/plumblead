  • Customer has eczema or sensitive skin

Seattle Softener Sizing

Seattle city water: 1-2 gpg hardness. Even smallest softener is oversized.

Example: 1.5 gpg × 300 gal/day × 7 days = 3,150 grains
→ A 32K softener would go 70+ days between regenerations (too long)
→ Set to time-clock mode: regen every 7-10 days regardless of capacity
⚠️ Critical: In soft water, resin stagnation causes bacterial growth → rotten egg smell. Set frequent regens (7-10 days max) or use sanitizing resin cleaner quarterly.

🔮 TAC — Template Assisted Crystallization (Salt-Free)

TAC in Seattle Context
⚠️ Seattle Reality: TAC systems prevent scale formation. But Seattle water is already soft (1-2 gpg) — scale is NOT a problem here. TAC systems have limited utility in the Seattle market for municipal water customers.

When TAC makes sense in Seattle:

  • Well water with elevated hardness (rare, but exists in rural areas)
  • Commercial/industrial applications with specific scale concerns
  • Customer philosophically opposed to salt-based softeners but has well water

Default recommendation: For Seattle municipal water, skip both softener AND TAC unless customer has specific preferences.

Lead Filtration — CRITICAL FOR PRE-1960 HOMES
🚨 Seattle has significant lead pipe risk in homes built before 1960. Lead service lines and lead solder were common. Always ask home age.

Solutions for lead:

  • Point-of-use: NSF 53 certified faucet filter or under-sink filter ($50-200)
  • Whole-house: KDF + catalytic carbon tank system ($800-1,200 installed)
  • Ultimate: RO system removes 95%+ lead ($350-600 installed)

Recommendation script: "Your home was built in [year]. Seattle homes from that era often have lead pipes or solder. Let's test your water and discuss lead filtration options to protect your family."
